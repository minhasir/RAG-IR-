Question: why was the civil rights act of 1866 made into an amendment to the constitution

Reference Answer: Senator Lyman Trumbull was the Senate sponsor of the Civil Rights Act of 1866 , and he argued that Congress had power to enact it in order to eliminate a discriminatory `` badge of servitude '' prohibited by the Thirteenth Amendment . Representative John Bingham , principal author of the first section of the Fourteenth Amendment , was one of several Republicans who believed ( prior to that Amendment ) that Congress lacked power to pass the 1866 Act . In the 20th century , the U.S. Supreme Court ultimately adopted Trumbull 's Thirteenth Amendment rationale for congressional power to ban racial discrimination by states and by private parties , in view of the fact that the Thirteenth Amendment does not require a state actor .

Okay, this is a layered question with a fascinating history! Let's break it down, addressing each part of your question about the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and its eventual incorporation into the 14th Amendment.

**1. Why was the Civil Rights Act of 1866 made?**

*   **Post-Civil War Context:** The Civil War (1861-1865) ended slavery, but it didn't automatically grant full citizenship and equal rights to newly freed African Americans.  Southern states, particularly, were enacting "Black Codes."
*   **Black Codes:** These laws were designed to restrict the freedoms and rights of African Americans. They varied by state but commonly included:
    *   Restricting their ability to own property.
    *   Limiting their ability to work in certain occupations.
    *   Restricting their right to vote.
    *   Imposing harsh penalties for minor offenses.
    *   Essentially, they aimed to recreate a system of near-slavery.
*   **Radical Republicans' Response:**  Radical Republicans in Congress (like Thaddeus Stevens and Charles Sumner) were deeply concerned about these Black Codes. They believed that the federal government had a responsibility to protect the rights of newly freed slaves and ensure they were treated as citizens.
*   **The Act's Provisions:** The Civil Rights Act of 1866 declared that all persons born in the United States (except Native Americans) were citizens, regardless of race or previous condition of slavery. It granted them the right to:
    *   Enter into contracts.
    *   Sue in federal court.
    *   Purchase and lease property.
    *   Be equal to white citizens in the courts.

**2. Why was the Civil Rights Act of 1866 challenged and ultimately incorporated into the 14th Amendment?**

*   **Presidential Veto:** President Andrew Johnson, a Southern Democrat who opposed the goals of Reconstruction, vetoed the Civil Rights Act of 1866. He believed it was an overreach of federal power and an infringement on states' rights.
*   **Congressional Override:**  The Radical Republicans in Congress were able to override Johnson's veto, making the Act law. However, they recognized that a simple act of Congress could be challenged in the Supreme Court and potentially struck down.
*   **The Supreme Court's Concerns (and the need for a Constitutional Amendment):**  The Radical Republicans feared the Supreme Court would rule that the Civil Rights Act of 1866 was unconstitutional, arguing that Congress did not have the power to legislate on matters of citizenship and equal protection.  They were right to be concerned.
*   **The 14th Amendment as a Solution:** To solidify the rights guaranteed by the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and prevent future challenges, Congress proposed the 14th Amendment to the Constitution.
*   **Key Provisions of the 14th Amendment:**
    *   **Citizenship Clause:**  Explicitly defines citizenship, ensuring that all persons born or naturalized in the United States are citizens. This superseded the Citizenship Clause of the Civil Rights Act.
    *   **Equal Protection Clause:**  Guarantees that states cannot "deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws." This is the cornerstone of modern civil rights law.
    *   **Due Process Clause:**  Guarantees that states cannot deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.

**3.  So, to directly answer your final question: "why was the civil rights act of 1866 made into an amendment to the constitution?"**

The Civil Rights Act of 1866 wasn't *made* into an amendment. Rather, the 14th Amendment was *drafted to enshrine the principles and protections* of the Civil Rights Act of 1866 within the Constitution itself.  It was a strategic move to ensure the long-term viability and enforceability of those rights, preventing them from being easily overturned by the Supreme Court or future Congresses. The 14th Amendment provided a much stronger constitutional foundation for civil rights than a simple act of Congress could have.



**In essence, the Civil Rights Act of 1866 was the catalyst, and the 14th Amendment was the constitutional anchor.**
