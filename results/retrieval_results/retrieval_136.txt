This is less likely to be a completely new audience unfamiliar with the basic story of Jesus.

**2. The Broader Audience (Later Expansion): All Christians**

*   **Universal Themes:** While initially aimed at Jewish Christians, the Gospel of John contains universal themes of God's love, salvation, and eternal life that would appeal to all Christians, regardless of their background.
*   **Early Church Usage:** The Gospel of John was quickly adopted and used by the wider Christian community, becoming one of the four canonical Gospels.
*   **Translation and Dissemination:** As Christianity spread beyond its Jewish roots, the Gospel was translated into Greek and other languages, reaching a much broader audience.

**3. The Author's Purpose (Beyond the Audience):**

*   **To Establish Jesus's Divinity:** A primary purpose of the Gospel of John is to demonstrate that Jesus is the divine Son of God, the Word (Logos) who existed with God from the beginning. This is a central theme that distinguishes John from the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, and Luke).
*   **To Provide Evidence for Faith:** John presents "signs" (miracles) that are intended to convince readers that Jesus is who he claims to be. These signs are not just miraculous events but are meant to reveal Jesus's divine nature.
*   **To Encourage Discipleship:** The Gospel calls readers to believe in Jesus, follow him, and abide in his love. It emphasizes the importance of a personal relationship with Jesus.

**4. The "Gospel of John" - Who Wrote It?**

*   **Traditional Attribution:** The Gospel is traditionally attributed to John, one of the twelve apostles of Jesus.
*   **Authorship Debate:** Modern scholarship debates the precise authorship. Some scholars believe that the Gospel was written by a community of followers of John, or that it was based on the teachings and traditions associated with John. Regardless of the precise authorship, the Gospel is considered to be a reliable witness to the life and teachings of Jesus.

**In Summary:**

The Gospel of John was likely initially written for Jewish Christians who were already somewhat familiar with Jesus but needed further conviction of his divinity. However, its universal themes and enduring message have made it a source of faith and inspiration for Christians of all backgrounds throughout the centuries.
